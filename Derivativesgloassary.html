Derivatives Glossary

  1. Derivative: A financial instrument whose value is derived from the value of an underlying asset.
  2. Underlying Asset: The asset on which the value of a derivative is based, such as a stock, commodity, or currency.
  3. Futures Contract: A contract that obligates the buyer to purchase an underlying asset at a specific price on a specific date in the future.
  4. Options Contract: A contract that gives the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specific price on or before a specific date in the future.
  5. Call Option: An options contract that gives the buyer the right to buy an underlying asset at a specific price on or before a specific date in the future.
  6. Put Option: An options contract that gives the buyer the right to sell an underlying asset at a specific price on or before a specific date in the future.
  7. Strike Price: The price at which an underlying asset can be bought or sold under the terms of an options contract.
  8. Premium: The price paid by the buyer for an options contract.
  9. Expiration Date: The date on which an options contract expires and becomes invalid.
  10. In-the-Money: An options contract that has intrinsic value, meaning that the strike price is favorable compared to the current market price of the underlying asset.
  11. Out-of-the-Money: An options contract that has no intrinsic value, meaning that the strike price is unfavorable compared to the current market price of the underlying asset.
  12. Hedging: The practice of using derivatives to offset the risk of an adverse price movement in an underlying asset.
  13. Swaps: An agreement between two parties to exchange cash flows based on a notional amount, often used to manage interest rate or currency risks.
  14. Forward Contract: A contract that obligates the buyer to purchase an underlying asset at a specific price on a specific date in the future, similar to a futures contract but not traded on an exchange.
  15. Collateral: Assets pledged by a borrower to secure a loan or other obligation, often used in derivatives trading to manage counterparty risk.
